I like important problems or interesting problems. If they happen to be easily solvable as well, then I’d be happy about it.

    While its true, important problems are often hard, its important to draw the delineation.

    I spent a lot of my time between the ages of 18–25 searching for hard problems. I worked on a tropical geometry problem one summer. Another summer was spent working in cryptography. My last summer focused on optimization. I attended quantum information seminars and tried to read statistical physics papers. I spent a school year doing a reading course in dynamical systems. Upon graduating college, as a research engineer I worked in control theory, quantum arithmetic, and embedded systems. You name it, I probably spent some time in it.

    Now, I wouldn’t give up that random walk for anything, I was blessed with meeting great peers and wonderful mentors through many of those experiences. Still, most of it left me feeling unfilled and more lost as to what my purpose should be.

    I realized I was searching for hard problems to fix my inner child that was bullied by teachers for being too slow or not smart enough. I searched for hard problems to prove to myself that I could do hard things, but I never really cared about the problems themselves. They were uninteresting and not important in the ways that matter to me. Consequently, I often had minimal impact on projects – my heart was never in it. I don’t have enough drive to work on the hard and boring.

    I will no longer be working on hard problems for hardness sake. My focus will now be shifted towards important problems.

    So whats an important problem? I’m not sure yet, but I have found that I’m intrigued by solutions that

    • Saves people time or
    • Allows them to make a money or
    • Educates/Informs them properly

    My new goal is to find the problem space that makes my heart flutter.

    I’m off on another walk, but this time a little less random, as I’m more clear on what it is I’m seeking.
